# SECTION 1: SENTENCE COMPLETION
### 20 Questions | 25 seconds each | Fill ONE word

---

## What It Tests
- Grammar correctness
- Vocabulary in context
- Logical fit of word in sentence

---

## TRICK 1 — Part of Speech First
Before thinking of word, identify WHAT type of word is needed:
- Blank after "the/a/an" → **Noun or Adjective**
- Blank after "is/was/are/were" → **Adjective or Noun**
- Blank after subject → **Verb**
- Blank after "very/so/quite/too" → **Adjective or Adverb**
- Blank ending in blank+ly → **Adverb likely**

---

## TRICK 2 — Tone Detection
Every sentence has a tone — positive or negative. Match the blank to tone.

- "Despite his efforts, the project was a ___" → negative word (failure, disaster)
- "Her performance was so ___ that everyone applauded" → positive word (brilliant, outstanding)
- "Although he seemed confident, he was actually ___" → contrast = negative (nervous, afraid)

**Contrast signal words:** although, despite, however, yet, but, even though → blank = opposite of rest of sentence

**Support signal words:** because, since, therefore, as a result, moreover → blank = same tone as sentence

---

## TRICK 3 — Elimination by Grammar
- If sentence is past tense → blank must be past tense verb
- If sentence has "has/have" → blank = past participle (V3)
- If "he/she/it" subject present tense → verb needs +s

---

## TRICK 4 — Collocations (Word Pairs)
Some words always go together — memorize these:

| Before blank | Likely word |
|-------------|-------------|
| make a ___ | decision, difference, mistake, effort |
| take ___ | action, steps, initiative, responsibility |
| pay ___ | attention, respect, tribute |
| come to a ___ | conclusion, decision, agreement |
| ___ decision | wise, final, hasty, informed |
| ___ silence | complete, deafening, awkward |
| ___ victory | decisive, hard-fought, resounding |
| ___ effort | sincere, tremendous, concerted |

---

## TRICK 5 — 25 Seconds Strategy
- Read sentence once fully
- Identify blank position and tone
- Think of word in 10 seconds
- Type and move — don't second-guess

---

## HIGH-FREQUENCY WORDS FOR SENTENCE COMPLETION

**Positive words:** diligent, eloquent, proficient, commendable, resilient, exemplary, meticulous, prudent, amiable, candid

**Negative words:** negligent, incompetent, erratic, dubious, ambiguous, futile, redundant, oblivious, reckless, complacent

**Neutral/Action:** implemented, facilitated, initiated, collaborated, emphasized, acknowledged, demonstrated, illustrated

---

## GRAMMAR RULES FOR SENTENCE COMPLETION

**Subject-Verb Agreement**
- Singular subject → singular verb (adds -s)
- "The team **is**" not "The team are" (collective noun = singular)
- "Each/Every/Either/Neither" → always singular verb
- "None of the students **has**" (formal) or "have" (informal)

**Tense Consistency**
- If sentence starts in past → blank must be past
- "He was running when she ___" → arrived (simple past)
- Conditional: "If I were you, I would ___" → base verb

**Articles**
- Before vowel sound → "an" (an hour, an honest man)
- Before consonant sound → "a" (a university — 'yu' sound)
- Specific/known → "the"

**Preposition Collocations**
- Afraid **of**, Angry **with** (person) / Angry **at** (situation)
- Agree **with** (person) / Agree **to** (proposal)
- Responsible **for**, Depend **on**, Consist **of**
- Interested **in**, Good **at**, Proud **of**

# SECTION 2: PASSAGE RECALL
### 4+4 Questions | 30 seconds read | 90 seconds write

---

## What It Tests
- Reading comprehension speed
- Memory of key facts
- Ability to reconstruct content accurately

---

## THE 30-SECOND READING STRATEGY

**First 5 seconds:** Read first sentence — this is usually the MAIN IDEA
**Next 20 seconds:** Scan for: Who, What, When, Where, Why, How
**Last 5 seconds:** Read last sentence — usually CONCLUSION or KEY POINT

**What to mentally note:**
- Names (people, places, organizations)
- Numbers (dates, statistics, quantities)
- Action words (what happened)
- Cause-effect relationships

---

## MEMORY TRICKS FOR 30 SECONDS

**SWCN Method** (Say While reading, Connect Numbers)
- As you read, mentally say key facts
- Numbers: attach to something (44 million = India's half population)
- Names: visualize the person doing the action

**Chunking**
- Don't read word by word
- Read in chunks of 4-5 words
- Chunk 1 = topic, Chunk 2 = action, Chunk 3 = result

**First-Last Rule**
- First sentence = topic/main idea
- Last sentence = conclusion/impact
- These two are MOST likely to be asked

---

## THE 90-SECOND WRITING STRATEGY

**First 10 seconds:** Recall what you remember — mentally list 3-4 key points

**Template to follow:**
```
The passage discusses [MAIN TOPIC].
[KEY FACT 1 with detail].
[KEY FACT 2 with detail].
[CONCLUSION or IMPACT stated in passage].
```

**What scorers look for:**

- Key facts present (names, numbers, events)
- Logical flow
- NOT word-for-word copying — your own words
- Accuracy of content, not language perfection

---

## COMMON PASSAGE TYPES & WHAT TO FOCUS ON

**Business/Corporate passage:**
- Focus: company name, what they did, result (profit/loss/growth %), who was involved

**Science/Technology passage:**
- Focus: what was discovered/invented, who, when, what it does, impact

**Social/Current affairs passage:**
- Focus: issue, statistics, government action, affected group, solution mentioned

**Historical passage:**
- Focus: who, what event, when (year), consequence

---

## PASSAGE RECALL — PRACTICE METHOD

**Daily practice drill:**
- Read any paragraph for 30 seconds
- Close it
- Write everything you remember in 90 seconds
- Compare with original
- Track: how many key facts captured?

**Target:** Capture minimum 5-6 key facts in 30 seconds

# SECTION 3: EMAIL WRITING
### 1 Question | 9 Minutes | Minimum 100 words

---

## What It Tests
- Professional writing ability
- Grammar and sentence structure
- Appropriate tone and format
- Completeness of content

---

## PROFESSIONAL EMAIL STRUCTURE (Always follow this)

```
Subject: [Clear, specific subject line]

Dear [Name/Sir/Ma'am/Team],

[PARAGRAPH 1 — Opening: State purpose in 1-2 lines]

[PARAGRAPH 2 — Body: Main content, details, explanation]

[PARAGRAPH 3 — Action/Request: What you need or what you will do]

[PARAGRAPH 4 — Closing: Polite closing line]

Regards,
[Your Name]
[Designation if given]
```

---

## OPENING LINES (memorize 3-4)

- "I am writing to bring to your attention..."
- "I am writing with reference to..."
- "I hope this email finds you well. I am writing to..."
- "This is to inform you that..."
- "I would like to request..."
- "I am pleased to inform you that..."
- "I regret to inform you that..."

---

## BODY PARAGRAPH PHRASES

**Adding information:**
- "Furthermore...", "In addition to this...", "It is also important to note that..."

**Explaining reason:**
- "This is due to...", "The reason for this is...", "As a result of..."

**Giving details:**
- "Specifically...", "To elaborate...", "In this regard..."

---

## CLOSING LINES (memorize 2-3)

- "I look forward to your prompt response."
- "Please feel free to contact me should you require any further information."
- "I would appreciate your earliest attention to this matter."
- "Thank you for your time and consideration."
- "Kindly revert at your earliest convenience."

---

## EMAIL TYPES & TEMPLATES

**Type 1 — Request Email**
```
Subject: Request for [specific thing]

Dear [Name],

I am writing to request [specific thing]. 
[Reason why you need it — 2-3 sentences].
[How it will help / urgency — 1-2 sentences].
I would be grateful if you could [specific action] by [date if applicable].

Thank you for your consideration.

Regards,
[Name]
```

**Type 2 — Complaint Email**
```
Subject: Complaint Regarding [Issue]

Dear [Name/Team],

I am writing to formally bring to your attention 
an issue I have encountered regarding [topic].
[Describe problem clearly — what happened, when, impact].
[What you expected vs what happened].
I request you to [specific resolution] at the earliest.

I trust this matter will receive your immediate attention.

Regards,
[Name]
```

**Type 3 — Apology / Explanation Email**
```
Subject: Apology for [Issue]

Dear [Name],

I am writing to sincerely apologize for [issue].
[What happened — brief explanation].
[Why it happened — reason, not excuse].
[What steps you are taking to ensure it doesn't repeat].
I assure you this will not happen again and 
I remain committed to [relevant responsibility].

Regards,
[Name]
```

**Type 4 — Information / Update Email**
```
Subject: Update on [Topic]

Dear [Name/Team],

I am writing to provide you with an update on [topic].
[Current status — what has been done].
[What is pending and timeline].
[Any issues or support needed].
Please let me know if you require any further details.

Regards,
[Name]
```

**Type 5 — Invitation Email**
```
Subject: Invitation to [Event]

Dear [Name],

I am pleased to invite you to [event name] 
scheduled on [date] at [time] at [venue/platform].
[Purpose of event — 2 sentences].
[What attendee needs to do — confirm, prepare, bring].
We look forward to your presence.

Regards,
[Name]
```

## GRAMMAR RULES FOR EMAIL (Most Common Errors)

**Tense Consistency**
- Formal emails → simple present or simple past
- Never mix tenses in same paragraph

**Active vs Passive**
- Emails prefer active voice
- "I will send the report" NOT "The report will be sent by me"
- Passive acceptable for formal requests: "The matter is requested to be resolved"

**Common Grammar Mistakes to Avoid**
- "I am having a question" → "I have a question"
- "Please do the needful" → acceptable in Indian English but "Please take necessary action" is better
- "Revert back" → just "revert" or "respond"
- "Kindly do it as soon as possible" → "at your earliest convenience"
- Starting sentence with "And" or "But" → avoid in formal email
- "I am sorry for the inconvenience caused" → add specifics

---

## VOCABULARY FOR EMAILS

**Formal alternatives:**
- Ask → Request, Enquire
- Tell → Inform, Notify, Advise
- Need → Require
- Help → Assist, Support
- Problem → Issue, Concern, Matter
- Fix → Resolve, Rectify, Address
- Think → Believe, Consider
- Show → Demonstrate, Illustrate
- Want → Wish, Would like
- Sorry → Apologize, Regret

If struggling to reach 100 words:
- Add one more detail in body paragraph
- Expand the closing paragraph
- Add "I would like to emphasize that..." sentence
- Add context sentence in opening

**Count tip:** 5 lines ≈ 50 words. So minimum 10 lines needed.

## READING COMPREHENSION — Quick Strategy

**Step 1:** Read all questions FIRST (30 seconds)
**Step 2:** Read passage — underline as you read what relates to questions
**Step 3:** Answer factual questions directly from passage
**Step 4:** Inference questions — eliminate wrong options

**Types of Questions:**
- Main idea → usually in first/last paragraph
- Specific detail → scan for keywords from question
- Inference → what author implies but doesn't state
- Tone → look at adjectives used by author
- Title → must cover ENTIRE passage, not one part

---

## ERROR SPOTTING — All Grammar Rules

**Subject-Verb Agreement**
- "One of the boys **has**" (not have — subject is "one")
- "The quality of mangoes **is**" (subject = quality)
- "Neither of them **is**" (neither = singular)
- "News **is**" (uncountable → singular)
- "Mathematics **is**" (subject ending in -ics = singular)

**Tense Errors**
- "Since" → present perfect: "I have lived here since 2010"
- "For" → present perfect or past: "I have worked here for 5 years"
- "Yesterday/Last/Ago" → simple past only, never present perfect
- "Just/Already/Yet/Ever/Never" → present perfect

**Article Errors**
- "The + superlative": "the best"
- "The + unique thing": "the sun, the moon"
- "A/An" before singular countable noun when first mention
- No article: uncountable (water, advice), plural general (dogs are loyal)

**Preposition Errors**
- Married **to** (not with)
- Die **of** disease (not from)
- Die **from** injury/accident
- Suffer **from**
- Comply **with**
- Immune **to**
- Cope **with**

**Pronoun Errors**
- "Between you and **me**" (not I — object of preposition)
- "Everyone must bring **their** book" (everyone = singular but their acceptable)
- Reflexive: "He hurt **himself**" not "He hurt him"

## SENTENCE REARRANGEMENT (PARA JUMBLES)

**Strategy:**
1. Find the OPENING sentence: introduces topic, no pronoun reference, no connector
2. Find CLOSING sentence: conclusion, summary, no new information
3. Find mandatory pairs: "This/These/That" → refers to something just mentioned
4. Connect using: First → Then → However → Therefore → Finally

**Signal words:**
- First sentence signals: "The/A [topic]...", "Recently...", "In today's world..."
- Last sentence signals: "Thus/Therefore/In conclusion/Hence/Finally..."
- Middle sentence signals: "However/Moreover/Furthermore/In addition/On the other hand..."
